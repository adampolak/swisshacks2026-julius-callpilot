# global macroeconomic overview (2024–2025)

A one-to-two line macroeconomic snapshot for 100 of the world's most significant economies, ordered roughly by GDP size.

---

## 1. united states
The world's largest economy (~$28 trillion GDP) continues to grow above trend, driven by robust consumer spending and AI-led investment, though the Fed has begun cautious rate cuts as inflation eases toward the 2% target.

## 2. china
Growth has slowed to around 4–5% amid a prolonged property-sector crisis, weak consumer confidence, and deflationary pressures, while the government rolls out targeted fiscal stimulus to meet its official growth target.

## 3. germany
Europe's largest economy stagnated through 2024, weighed down by high energy costs, weak industrial output, and sluggish Chinese demand for its exports, with the government debating fiscal loosening within tight constitutional debt-brake rules.

## 4. japan
Japan has exited decades of deflation and the Bank of Japan has begun normalising rates, but growth remains modest, constrained by demographic decline and a still-fragile wage-price cycle.

## 5. india
The standout large-economy growth story of the decade, expanding at roughly 7% annually, driven by manufacturing investment, digital infrastructure, and a young, rising middle class — though fiscal consolidation and rural demand remain challenges.

## 6. united kingdom
Sluggish growth and stubborn services inflation have forced the Bank of England into a slow easing cycle; the economy faces long-run productivity headwinds and elevated public debt following the post-pandemic fiscal expansion.

## 7. france
France faces a widening fiscal deficit (above 5% of GDP) and political gridlock that has complicated budget consolidation, even as consumption holds up and tourism continues to boost the services sector.

## 8. brazil
After a surprisingly strong 2023–24 cycle, Brazil is grappling with a rising fiscal deficit, elevated interest rates, and currency pressure; the Lula government's spending plans have rattled bond markets.

## 9. italy
Growth is sluggish but the Meloni government has managed to narrow the fiscal deficit; the economy benefits from strong tourism and PNRR-funded investment, though public debt above 140% of GDP remains a structural vulnerability.

## 10. canada
Cooling from a post-pandemic boom, Canada faces a housing affordability crisis and slowing domestic demand; the Bank of Canada was among the first G7 central banks to cut rates aggressively in 2024.

## 11. south korea
A highly export-dependent economy navigating a semiconductor cycle upturn while dealing with weak domestic consumption, a sharp property-market correction, and rising household debt.

## 12. russia
Experiencing an overheated war economy with very low unemployment and elevated inflation; Western sanctions have redirected trade toward China and the Gulf, but long-run structural costs and capital flight are accumulating.

## 13. australia
Sustained by commodity exports and immigration-driven population growth, Australia nonetheless faces a cost-of-living squeeze, a sluggish housing construction sector, and a central bank that has been slower than peers to cut rates.

## 14. spain
One of the eurozone's fastest-growing large economies in 2024, buoyed by strong tourism, energy transition investment, and recovering real wages, though youth unemployment and housing affordability remain persistent concerns.

## 15. mexico
Benefits from nearshoring as US firms diversify supply chains away from China, supporting manufacturing investment in the north; inflation has eased but Banxico is cautious, and fiscal risks from energy-sector SOEs persist.

## 16. indonesia
Steady growth of around 5%, underpinned by commodity revenues (nickel, coal) and domestic consumption; President Prabowo's administration is pursuing ambitious infrastructure and food-security spending, raising fiscal scrutiny.

## 17. netherlands
A highly open, trade-dependent economy feeling the drag of global weakness in goods trade; fiscal surpluses are narrowing and housing supply constraints continue to fuel high prices.

## 18. saudi arabia
Non-oil growth is robust as Vision 2030 diversification spending accelerates, but the overall fiscal balance is sensitive to oil prices, and mega-project ambitions carry execution risk.

## 19. turkey
Inflation has been brought down from triple digits following a dramatic U-turn to orthodox monetary policy in 2023–24, with very high interest rates; the lira has stabilised but the disinflation process is slow and costly for households.

## 20. switzerland
A small, wealthy, highly competitive economy with very low inflation and a strong franc; growth is modest, driven by pharma, financial services, and precision manufacturing, with the SNB having been the first major central bank to cut rates.

## 21. taiwan
Riding a global AI and chip supercycle, Taiwan's semiconductor-driven economy posted strong growth in 2024; geopolitical risk related to cross-strait tensions remains the dominant long-run vulnerability.

## 22. poland
One of Central Europe's most dynamic economies, benefiting from EU cohesion funds, nearshoring from Western Europe, and strong domestic consumption; inflation has eased but remains above the NBP target.

## 23. sweden
After a sharp housing market correction and a mild recession in 2023–24, Sweden is recovering slowly; the Riksbank cut rates decisively and the krona has faced depreciation pressure.

## 24. belgium
A high-debt, high-tax economy with a structurally wide fiscal deficit; growth is subdued and the newly formed government faces pressure from Brussels to consolidate public finances.

## 25. argentina
In the midst of a severe economic adjustment under President Milei, who slashed public spending and devalued the currency in late 2023; inflation has peaked above 200% but is now falling rapidly, at great social cost.

## 26. norway
Buffered by its sovereign wealth fund, Norway enjoys fiscal comfort and low unemployment; the oil-and-gas sector remains strong, though the krone has been weak and Norges Bank has been cautious on rate cuts.

## 27. austria
A small, open, manufacturing-oriented economy closely tied to Germany's business cycle and thus also in a mild industrial recession; fiscal consolidation is underway after post-pandemic expansion.

## 28. united arab emirates
Rapid non-oil growth driven by trade, tourism, real estate, and financial services; Abu Dhabi's fiscal buffers are vast, and Dubai continues to attract capital and talent flows from across the globe.

## 29. nigeria
Africa's largest economy by GDP struggles with chronic inflation above 30%, a sharp naira devaluation following subsidy removal, and oil production below capacity; reform momentum is positive but growth barely keeps pace with population.

## 30. israel
The conflict that began in October 2023 has imposed severe economic costs — falling output, surging defence spending, disrupted tourism and FDI — though tech-sector resilience and fiscal buffers have cushioned the blow.

## 31. malaysia
A diversified Southeast Asian economy benefiting from semiconductor demand and foreign investment shifts; inflation is low and growth solid, with ringgit weakness the main near-term concern.

## 32. singapore
A global financial and logistics hub posting healthy growth, though sensitive to global trade volumes; the MAS has maintained a tight exchange-rate policy to keep inflation anchored.

## 33. philippines
One of ASEAN's fastest-growing economies (~6%), driven by consumer spending, remittances, and a booming BPO sector; elevated food inflation and external debt are risk factors.

## 34. denmark
A high-income economy with strong pharma exports (notably GLP-1 drugs from Novo Nordisk, which have single-handedly boosted national growth statistics); fiscal surpluses are large and unemployment is at historic lows.

## 35. iran
Under heavy US sanctions, Iran's economy is distorted, inflation is very high, and the rial has collapsed in value; oil exports continue via informal channels, supporting the government but not the broader population.

## 36. colombia
Moderate growth of around 2–3%, with the Petro government's expansionary fiscal stance in tension with a cautious Banco de la República; oil revenues remain key but decarbonisation policy uncertainty weighs on investment.

## 37. south africa
Persistent electricity shortages and logistics bottlenecks have trapped growth below 1%; the GNU government formed in 2024 has raised hopes for reform but structural challenges are deep.

## 38. egypt
A severe balance-of-payments crisis was partially resolved via an expanded IMF programme and Gulf financial support in 2024; inflation remains very high and the pound has sharply depreciated, squeezing living standards.

## 39. romania
Fast-growing by EU standards but with one of the bloc's widest fiscal deficits (~8% of GDP), Romania faces pressure from the European Commission and must tighten policy without undermining growth.

## 40. vietnam
A major manufacturing hub attracting FDI diverted from China; growth is solid at ~6–7%, though the economy is heavily export-dependent and vulnerable to US trade policy shifts.

## 41. chile
Moderate growth following the commodity super-cycle peak; fiscal consolidation is underway and copper revenues remain key, while political uncertainty around a new constitution has damped investment.

## 42. czech republic
A small, open, manufacturing-heavy economy tightly linked to Germany; growth has been weak but inflation has fallen sharply from its post-energy-crisis peak, allowing the Czech National Bank to cut rates.

## 43. hungary
High inflation relative to EU peers, a weak forint, and an unusually wide fiscal deficit have constrained growth; the Orbán government's political standoff with the EU over cohesion funds has cut off a key investment source.

## 44. portugal
Outperforming the eurozone average with solid tourism revenues, strong FDI, and improving public finances; the fiscal balance has moved into surplus and public debt is on a declining trajectory.

## 45. greece
A dramatic fiscal turnaround story: from near-default a decade ago to primary surpluses today; the economy continues to grow above the eurozone average, driven by tourism, shipping, and rising investment.

## 46. new zealand
Fell into recession in 2023–24 as aggressive rate hikes slowed the housing-driven economy; the RBNZ has begun cutting rates and a gradual recovery is underway.

## 47. kazakhstan
A resource-rich economy benefiting from elevated commodity prices and transit trade rerouted through Central Asia post-Russia sanctions; inflation has eased but governance and diversification challenges persist.

## 48. peru
Political instability has suppressed investment and growth, though the commodity export base (copper, gold) provides a revenue floor; poverty reduction has stalled.

## 49. finland
Like Germany, Finland has experienced a mild recession driven by weak real estate, higher interest rates, and exposure to Russian trade cutoff; fiscal consolidation is underway under a centre-right government.

## 50. iraq
Oil revenues fund most of the government budget, leaving the economy highly exposed to price volatility; reconstruction needs are vast, corruption is entrenched, and non-oil private sector development is minimal.

## 51. ukraine
Wartime economy sustained by massive Western financial transfers; GDP has partially rebounded from the sharp 2022 collapse but the war effort consumes an enormous share of output and external debt is mounting rapidly.

## 52. slovakia
A eurozone member heavily dependent on automotive manufacturing; struggling with the shift to electric vehicles and broad industrial weakness echoing Germany's slowdown.

## 53. ethiopia
Among Africa's fastest-growing large economies, though the Tigray conflict inflicted deep damage; government debt has been restructured under the G20 Common Framework, and inflation is being gradually contained.

## 54. kenya
Growth of around 5%, driven by services and tech; but a heavy debt burden and a controversial IMF-linked finance bill sparked social unrest in 2024, and the shilling has been under pressure.

## 55. morocco
Steady growth anchored by strong remittances, tourism, and phosphate exports; the country is positioning itself as a manufacturing gateway between Europe and Africa, attracting significant automotive FDI.

## 56. cuba
Deep economic crisis with severe shortages of food, fuel, and medicine; the end of Venezuelan oil subsidies, US sanctions, and a failed electricity system have pushed GDP sharply lower and emigration has surged.

## 57. angola
An oil-dependent economy undergoing an IMF-backed reform programme; the kwanza has depreciated sharply, inflation is high, and the government is attempting to diversify revenue while managing a heavy debt burden.

## 58. ecuador
Dollarised economy facing acute fiscal stress and security crisis; the government of President Noboa has implemented austerity and is negotiating debt relief, while declining oil output erodes the revenue base.

## 59. sri lanka
Completing a historic debt restructuring after the 2022 sovereign default; growth is recovering and inflation has fallen sharply, but living standards remain well below pre-crisis levels and external debt remains high.

## 60. ghana
Completed a domestic debt exchange and is working through an IMF programme after its 2022 debt crisis; inflation is still very high and the cedi has suffered major depreciation, though the worst of the crisis appears to have passed.

## 61. croatia
A eurozone member since 2023, growing steadily above the EU average; tourism is a major driver and the fiscal position is sound, though the country remains reliant on EU cohesion funds for investment.

## 62. myanmar
Economic collapse since the 2021 military coup, compounded by civil war; the kyat has lost most of its value, foreign investment has fled, and basic services have deteriorated sharply.

## 63. tanzania
Solid growth of around 5%, underpinned by gold and tourism; political stability and infrastructure investment are positives, though the current account deficit is wide and external financing needs are significant.

## 64. costa rica
One of Latin America's stronger performers, with a diversified export base in medical devices, tech services, and tourism; the fiscal deficit has been narrowed and the colón has been stable.

## 65. bulgaria
Growing faster than the EU average, with a strong fiscal position and low public debt; the country is on track to adopt the euro in 2025, imposing additional fiscal discipline requirements.

## 66. ivory coast
West Africa's largest economy, growing robustly on cocoa, cashew exports, and infrastructure investment; the CFA franc peg provides monetary stability, but high public investment is widening the fiscal deficit.

## 67. dominican republic
The Caribbean's largest economy, with sustained high growth driven by tourism, remittances, and a booming free-trade-zone manufacturing sector.

## 68. uzbekistan
Fast-growing Central Asian economy (5–7% annually) undergoing market reforms; trade corridor benefits from Russia-sanctions rerouting, and gold exports support the current account, though institutional reform progress is uneven.

## 69. guatemala
Steady growth underpinned by remittances and agri-exports; the new Arévalo government has improved governance perceptions, though investment climate reforms are needed to broaden growth beyond the informal sector.

## 70. venezuela
A partial economic stabilisation has occurred as dollarisation took hold informally and oil production edged up; but the economy is a fraction of its 2012 peak and oil-dependent, with over 7 million citizens having emigrated.

## 71. nepal
Heavily remittance-dependent with persistent balance-of-payments pressure; energy and tourism are growth pillars but the economy remains underdiversified and politically fragile.

## 72. panama
A services and logistics hub growing above regional peers; the canal remains a critical revenue source, though the closure of a major copper mine following protests removed a significant export earner.

## 73. cameroon
Slow growth constrained by low oil production, security challenges in the Anglophone regions, and high debt; IMF programme conditions are providing a fiscal anchor.

## 74. bolivia
Acute foreign-exchange shortage and declining natural gas production are squeezing an economy that built its social-spending model on gas revenues; the boliviano's dollar peg is under stress.

## 75. paraguay
One of South America's quieter growth stories, with a stable economy anchored by soy and beef exports and a large informal sector; hydropower from Itaipú provides cheap energy and export revenue.

## 76. cambodia
A garment-and-tourism-driven economy recovering from the COVID shock; Chinese investment is significant but over-dependence on a narrow export base and governance risks remain concerns.

## 77. senegal
Entered a new growth phase with first oil and gas production beginning in 2024; the Faye government is reviewing major contracts, introducing uncertainty, but long-run hydrocarbon revenues could transform the fiscal outlook.

## 78. el salvador
Bitcoin adoption as legal tender has been more symbolic than transformative; an IMF deal reached in 2024 provides debt relief, and growth is supported by remittances and nearshoring interest, but fiscal vulnerabilities persist.

## 79. laos
Deep sovereign debt distress, with a large portion of debt owed to China; the kip has collapsed in value, inflation has been very high, and electricity export revenues from its hydropower dams are insufficient to service obligations.

## 80. luxembourg
A tiny but exceptionally wealthy financial centre with very high per-capita GDP; growth has slowed with the interest rate cycle dampening fund flows, but the fiscal position is strong.

## 81. nicaragua
Authoritarian consolidation under Ortega has chilled foreign investment; remittances from the large diaspora sustain household demand, while public institutions have been systematically weakened.

## 82. mongolia
A commodity-driven boom anchored by coal and copper exports to China; growth is strong but highly cyclical, and a narrow export base leaves the economy vulnerable to Chinese demand shifts.

## 83. honduras
Remittances are the backbone of the economy; political uncertainty and high debt have narrowed fiscal space, and violent crime continues to suppress investment and drive emigration.

## 84. trinidad and tobago
An energy-exporting twin-island economy benefiting from elevated LNG and petrochemical prices; revenue management and economic diversification beyond hydrocarbons remain long-standing policy challenges.

## 85. botswana
Diamond-dependent economy facing revenue pressure as De Beers production has slowed; the government is pursuing diversification while managing from a position of relatively strong fiscal reserves.

## 86. zambia
Completed a sovereign debt restructuring in 2023 after a protracted process; an IMF programme is underway and copper revenues provide the foundation for recovery, though social spending constraints are tight.

## 87. kyrgyzstan
Small, remittance-dependent economy benefiting from parallel trade flows rerouted through it due to Russia sanctions; gold production is the main export but the economy lacks diversification.

## 88. rwanda
One of Africa's growth leaders at 7–8% annually; the government's development agenda and strong institutions attract investment, though the economy is small, landlocked, and reliant on aid.

## 89. bahrain
A diversified Gulf economy relative to its neighbours, with financial services and aluminium alongside oil; ongoing fiscal consolidation is needed as oil revenues at current prices barely cover spending.

## 90. tajikistan
One of the world's poorest countries by per-capita income, heavily dependent on remittances from Russia (now under strain) and aluminium exports; infrastructure investment by China is deepening debt dependence.

## 91. haiti
In a state of deep humanitarian and economic collapse following gang takeover of Port-au-Prince; economic activity has virtually ceased in large parts of the country and the international security mission faces enormous challenges.

## 92. malawi
Among the world's poorest economies, facing foreign-exchange shortages, very high inflation, and acute food insecurity; an IMF programme is in place but structural transformation is needed to escape the poverty trap.

## 93. moldova
A small economy buffered by EU accession hopes and Western support; energy costs surged after Russia cut gas supplies, but EU trade integration and reform momentum are positives.

## 94. albania
Growing at around 3–4%, driven by tourism and energy exports (it is a net electricity exporter); EU accession negotiations are advancing and remittances from the large diaspora supplement domestic income.

## 95. north korea
Almost entirely opaque to external analysis; the economy is heavily militarised, sanctions-constrained, and famine-prone, with limited hard currency access and no meaningful foreign investment.

## 96. zimbabwe
After years of hyperinflation, the government introduced the ZiG (Zimbabwe Gold) currency in 2024; stabilisation is fragile, fiscal management remains weak, and the country remains cut off from international capital markets.

## 97. suriname
Emerging from a debt restructuring after the 2020–21 fiscal crisis; new offshore oil production coming online holds transformative potential if managed well, but institutional capacity is thin.

## 98. timor-leste
Almost entirely dependent on the Petroleum Fund, which is being drawn down faster than it is replenished; the government is urgently seeking to develop the Greater Sunrise gas field before the fund is exhausted.

## 99. bhutan
A small, hydropower-exporting Himalayan kingdom with strong fiscal ties to India; growth is constrained by scale, and a significant youth emigration wave ("brain drain") is an emerging structural concern.

## 100. maldives
A tourism-dependent island economy facing a severe debt crisis, with external obligations largely owed to China; foreign-exchange reserves are critically low and debt restructuring discussions are underway.

---

*data reflects conditions as of mid-2025. figures cited are approximate and draw on IMF World Economic Outlook, World Bank, and central bank sources.*
